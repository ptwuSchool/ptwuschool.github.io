Ronak



Before Action

by W.N. Hodgson



By all the glories of the day
And the cool evening's benison,
By that last sunset touch that lay
Upon the hills where day was done,
By beauty lavisghly outpoured
And blessings carelessly received,
By all the days that I have lived
Make me a soldier, Lord.
By all of man's hopes and fears,
And all the wonders poets sing,
The laughter of unclouded years,
And every sad and lovely thing;
By the romantic ages stored
With high endeavor that was his,
By all his mad catastrophes
Make me a man, O Lord.
I, that on my familiar hill
Saw with uncomprehending eyes
A hundred of Thy sunsets spill
Their fresh and sanguine sacrifice,
Ere the sun swings his noonday sword
Must say goodbye to all of this;--
By all delights that I shall miss,
Help me to die, O Lord.

Analysis

The tone of this poem is somber and melancholy. Hodgson uses anaphora by repeating "By" at the beginning of almost every phrase, and also uses parallel structure with "Make me a soldier, O Lord," and "Make me a man, O Lord." The final one, "Help me to die, O Lord," is a little different; more conclusive. This poem tells the story of a life. It starts off with ambitions to become a soldier and a man and ends with death and more appreciation for the beauty of life. Regret is prevalent throughout the poem with statements like "blessings carelessly received," but so is pride and glory. This poem romanticizes war, and treats dying for one's country as a "high endeavor" called for directly by God.

Back

by Wilfred Gibson



They ask me where I've been,
And what I've done and seen.
But what can I reply
Who know it wasn't I,
But someone just like me,
Who went across the sea
And with my head and hands
Killed men in foreign lands...
Though I must bear the blame,
Because he bore my name.

Analysis

*For ease of pronoun usage, the soldier has been assumed to be male*

This poem is my personal favorite. The soldier speaking uses a sort of metaphor to explain the feeling he's left with after the war. He did unspeakable things during the war that he regrets, but he feels disconnected from his actions. He finds it hard to believe that he is the same person who fought in the distant nightmare that was the war. I liked this poem because I think it very accurately sums up how a soldier must feel once war is over, but it does this in a way I've never seen before.


MCMXIV

by Philip Larkin



Those long uneven lines
Standing as patiently
As if they were stretched outside
The Oval or Villa Park,
The crowns of hats, the sun
On moustached archaic faces
Grinning as if it were all
An August Bank Holiday lark;

And the shut shops, the bleached
Established names on the sunblinds,
The farthings and sovereigns,
And dark-clothed children at play
Called after kings and queens,
The tin advertisements
For cocoa and twist, and the pubs
Wide open all day;

And the countryside not caring
The place-names all hazed over
With flowering grasses, and fields
Shadowing Domesday lines
Under wheats' restless silence;
The differently-dressed servants
With tiny rooms in huge houses,
The dust behind limousines;

Never such innocence,
Never before or since,
As changed itself to past
Without a word--the men
Leaving the gardens tidy,
The thousands of marriages
Lasting a little while longer:
Never such innocence again.

Analysis

The roman numeral MCMXIV is 1914, the year that WWI broke out. This poem is about how innocent and unknowing the people were when they left to fight. They had seen and fought in wars before, but WWI was different because of the advances in weaponry that had been made. Maxim machine guns and hidden mines along with trench warfare made the war much more gruesome. The poem is broken into four verses. The first verse explains how unassuming, naive and somewhat eager the soldiers were to go off to war. It gives the tone of being relaxed, in a way Larkin envies and also disapproves. The second and third describe the country, towns, and cities, void of the majority of men who are leaving to fight. Shops are empty, but otherwise, life will continue normally. The fourth and final verse is in sharp contrast regretful and reminiscent. Particularly ominous is the phrase, "The thousands of marriages Lasting a little while longer," which brings to mind the millions of marriages cut short abruptly by the war. Larkin makes it clear that the war left a permanent mark on the daily lives of everyone.

Original Poem

World War Won


As shrapnel flies above the trench
The flies engulf our putrid stench
Beside me, other Brits and French
Drop like flies as bullets drench

With worldwide mad disharmony
How is it that we keep the peace?
Blood and iron, all agree
As if they see no irony

With maxims as the guns to pick
To kill in conflicts with a click
Saving lives by taking lives
As if these two things don't conflict

Through deathly sadness, blood and gore
The weight of loss we proudly bore
But this the world chose to ignore
When they declared we won the war
